Topics

What Is Private Equity?

Private equity (PE) is a form of alternative investment where capital is raised from institutional and high-net-worth investors and deployed into private companies — businesses that are not listed on a public stock exchange. The goal is to generate superior risk-adjusted returns over a multi-year investment horizon, typically 10 years.

Unlike public market investing, PE investors take an active role in the companies they own — improving operations, replacing management, making bolt-on acquisitions, or restructuring balance sheets — before exiting via a sale or IPO.

Why Private Equity Exists

PE bridges the gap between businesses that need strategic capital and long-term partnerships, and investors seeking returns above what public markets typically offer. The illiquidity premium — extra return demanded for locking up capital — is the core economic rationale.

Key Fact: Global PE AUM (assets under management) exceeded $13 trillion in 2024, making it one of the largest asset classes in institutional investing.

The Private Equity Lifecycle
1
Fundraising (Year 0–1)
GP markets the fund to institutional LPs. A Limited Partnership Agreement (LPA) is signed. Target fund size is set (e.g., $500M). Capital commitments are secured but not yet drawn.
2
Investment Period (Year 1–5)
Capital is "called" from LPs as deals are found. Capital calls fund acquisitions. Typically 3–5 years; often 50% of commitments deployed in first 2 years.
3
Value Creation (Ongoing)
GP actively manages portfolio companies — cost reduction, revenue growth, add-on acquisitions, management changes, geographic expansion.
4
Harvesting / Exits (Year 4–10)
Portfolio companies are sold via strategic sale, secondary buyout, or IPO. Proceeds are distributed to LPs (and GP carry) as companies are exited.
5
Wind-Down (Year 10+)
Final audits, tax filings (K-1s), clawback calculations, fund dissolution. Some funds extend 1–2 years with LP consent.
Key Participants in a PE Fund

Understanding who does what in a PE fund is foundational. As an accountant, you will interact with nearly all of these parties directly or indirectly.

General Partner (GP)

The General Partner is the fund manager — typically the PE firm itself. The GP raises the fund, makes investment decisions, manages portfolio companies, and reports to LPs. The GP has unlimited liability for the fund's obligations (in a traditional LP structure), which is why GPs are often structured as LLCs.

  • Earns a management fee (typically 1.5–2.0% of committed capital per year)
  • Earns carried interest (typically 20% of profits above the hurdle rate)
  • Co-invests its own capital (typically 1–5% of the fund) to align incentives
Limited Partners (LPs)

LPs are the investors who provide the capital. They have limited liability — they can only lose what they invest. LPs are passive; they do not make investment decisions or manage the fund.

Fund Administrator

Third-party firm responsible for independent NAV calculation, capital account maintenance, capital call and distribution processing, financial statement preparation, and LP reporting. As a PE accountant, you will work closely with the fund admin or be the fund admin.

Auditors

Annual financial statements are audited by a Big 4 or specialized PE-focused audit firm (e.g., Deloitte, EY, KPMG, PwC, Grant Thornton). Auditors review fair value of investments, carried interest calculations, and LP capital account accuracy.

Legal Counsel

Drafts the Limited Partnership Agreement (LPA), side letters, subscription documents, and deal documentation. The LPA is the governing document for all economics — fees, carry, clawback, capital calls, distributions.

Valuation Specialists

For Level 3 investments without observable market prices, independent valuation firms are hired to opine on fair value. This is particularly important for auditors and for funds with quarterly reporting to LPs.

Accountant's Tip: The LPA is your bible. Every fee calculation, every waterfall step, every allocation rule is defined there. Read it carefully before you begin any calculation — fund structures vary significantly.
The Limited Partnership Agreement (LPA)

The LPA is the legal contract that governs every aspect of the fund. As a PE accountant, the LPA is your primary reference — it defines how you calculate fees, allocate income, and distribute proceeds.

Key LPA Provisions for Accountants
1. Capital Commitments & Calls

Defines each LP's total commitment (e.g., $50M), the investment period (typically 5 years), and the notice period for capital calls (typically 10 business days). Capital calls are the mechanism by which the GP draws down LP commitments.

2. Management Fee

Typically 2% per year on committed capital during the investment period, stepping down to 1.5% on invested capital (NAV of remaining portfolio) in the harvest period. Some LPAs offset fees against transaction fees or monitoring fees the GP earns from portfolio companies.

Annual Management Fee = Fee Rate × Fee Base Example: 2.0% × $500M committed = $10M/year Quarterly: $10M ÷ 4 = $2.5M per quarter
3. Preferred Return (Hurdle Rate)

The minimum annual return LPs must receive before the GP earns carry. Industry standard is 8% per annum, compounded annually. Some funds use a simple (non-compounding) hurdle.

4. Carried Interest

The GP's share of profits above the hurdle rate. Standard is 20% of profits. The LPA specifies whether carry is calculated on a deal-by-deal or whole-of-fund basis (see the Waterfall module).

5. Clawback

Requires the GP to return carried interest previously received if, at fund wind-down, total carry paid exceeds the GP's entitlement. The LPA specifies the clawback calculation, interest (if any), and tax gross-up provisions.

6. Key Man Provisions

Suspends the investment period if certain named individuals (key men) leave the GP. Protects LPs from investing capital with a materially changed team.

7. No-Fault Divorce / Removal

Allows a supermajority of LPs (typically 75%+ in interest) to remove the GP or terminate the fund. This is the LP's ultimate protective mechanism.

8. Side Letters

Bilateral agreements between the GP and specific LPs granting preferential terms — lower fees, co-investment rights, MFN (most favored nation) clauses, enhanced reporting. As accountant, you may need to track different fee structures per LP.

Watch Out: Side letters can materially change your management fee calculations for specific LPs. Always flag side letter LPs in your fee schedules and reconcile individually.
Fund Formation & Legal Structures
Common Legal Structures
Delaware Limited Partnership (Most Common – US)

The dominant structure for US PE funds. Flexible, well-precedented, and tax-transparent (pass-through). The fund files a partnership tax return (Form 1065); each LP receives a Schedule K-1 reporting their share of income, gains, and losses.

Cayman Islands Exempted Limited Partnership

Used for funds targeting international investors or for tax neutrality. Cayman funds are tax-exempt at the fund level. Often paired with a Delaware fund in a "parallel fund" structure.

Parallel Fund Structure

Two (or more) legal entities investing side by side in every deal in the same proportions. A US taxable entity (Delaware LP) for US tax-exempt investors, and a Cayman entity for non-US investors and US taxable investors who prefer offshore structures. Each fund has its own books, but economics are mirror-image.

Example Structure:
• Maple Capital Partners IV, L.P. (Delaware) → US pension funds, endowments
• Maple Capital Partners IV Offshore, L.P. (Cayman) → Sovereign wealth funds, foreign pensions
• Both funds invest side-by-side via a single operating entity (Maple Capital Partners IV Master, L.P.)
Luxembourg Structures (SCSp / SCA)

Common for European funds. The SCSp (Société en Commandite Spéciale) is Luxembourg's LP equivalent. AIFMD-compliant structures are required for marketing to European professional investors.

Umbrella / Master-Feeder Structures

Some large funds use a master fund where all portfolio investments are held, with multiple feeder funds (US, offshore, ERISA-friendly) that pool capital into the master. Simplifies deal execution but adds accounting complexity — the master fund consolidates portfolio companies while feeders hold only the master fund interest.

Entity Selection for the GP

The GP entity is typically a Delaware LLC or limited partnership. The GP LLC manages the fund and acts as the carried interest vehicle. Many GPs have a separate management company LLC that employs staff and charges management fees, and a separate GP LLC that receives carry — this separation has tax and legal rationale.

Accounting Standards for PE Funds

PE funds are investment companies under both US GAAP and IFRS. This classification triggers a specialized accounting model that is fundamentally different from operating company accounting. Understanding this distinction is the single most important conceptual leap for a junior PE accountant.

US GAAP: ASC 946

ASC 946 (Financial Services — Investment Companies) is the primary US GAAP standard for PE funds. The key principle:

Core Rule: Investment companies measure ALL investments at fair value, and ALL changes in fair value flow through the income statement (Statement of Operations). There is no OCI (Other Comprehensive Income) for investment companies.

This means unrealized appreciation (or depreciation) on portfolio company investments is recognized in net income every period — even if the investment has not been sold. This is dramatically different from operating company accounting where equity investments might be measured at cost or using the equity method.

IFRS: IAS 28 with FVTPL Election

Under IFRS, PE funds typically hold interests in portfolio companies. Normally IAS 28 (Investments in Associates) would require equity method accounting for significant influence (20%+ ownership). However, IAS 28 contains an exemption: venture capital organizations and similar entities may elect to measure such investments at Fair Value Through Profit or Loss (FVTPL). PE funds universally elect this exemption.

Investment Company Criteria (ASC 946-10-15)

To qualify as an investment company, an entity must meet all of the following:

  • It is an entity that is registered under the Investment Company Act of 1940, OR it obtains funds from investors to provide investment management services AND has the following attributes:
  • Investment activity: Business purpose is investing for returns (capital appreciation, investment income, or both)
  • Unit ownership: Ownership interests are in the form of equity or partnership interests
  • Pooling of funds: Multiple investors pool their funds
  • Reporting entity: Manages investments on behalf of investors
  • Fair value management: Manages and evaluates substantially all of its investments on a fair value basis
PE funds virtually always meet these criteria. The critical attribute is fair value management — the GP makes investment decisions based on fair values, and reports to LPs using fair values.

NAV & Capital Account Fundamentals

Net Asset Value (NAV) is the fund's total equity — what belongs to the partners after all liabilities. Every LP's capital account represents their individual share of NAV.

What Is NAV?
NAV = Total Assets at Fair Value − Total Liabilities NAV = Investments FV + Cash + Receivables − Management Fees Payable − Other Liabilities

NAV changes every period based on: capital calls received, investment appreciation/depreciation, income earned, expenses charged, and distributions made.

Capital Account Structure

Each LP (and the GP) has a capital account that tracks their individual position in the fund from inception to date. Capital accounts are always cumulative from inception — they do not reset each year.

Capital Account Rollforward (Cumulative from Inception): + Contributions (capital calls funded by this LP) + Allocated Net Income (realized gains + unrealized changes + income) − Allocated Expenses (management fees, fund expenses) − Distributions (return of capital + profit distributions) = Ending Capital Account Balance (= LP's share of NAV)

Unfunded Commitment

The difference between an LP's total commitment and the capital actually called to date. This is a contingent liability for the LP, not recorded on the fund's balance sheet (since it hasn't been called).

Unfunded Commitment = Total Commitment − Cumulative Capital Called Example: $10,000,000 − $8,000,000 = $2,000,000 remaining unfunded
GP Capital Account

The GP commits a percentage (typically 1–3%) of the fund and has its own capital account. The GP capital account is separate from carried interest. GP capital participates in gains and losses pro-rata, while carry is an additional profit allocation above the LP hurdle.

Key Point: A LP's capital account balance is their NAV — what they would receive if the fund were liquidated today at current fair values. This is the number LPs track closely for performance reporting.

Fair Value Framework — ASC 820 & IFRS 13

Fair value measurement is the cornerstone of PE fund accounting. Because virtually all portfolio investments are private (no quoted market price), determining fair value requires significant judgment and expertise.

Definition of Fair Value
ASC 820 / IFRS 13 Definition: Fair value is the price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to transfer a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date.

Key elements of this definition:

  • Exit price: The price to sell (not what you paid to buy)
  • Orderly transaction: Not a forced liquidation or distress sale
  • Market participants: Hypothetical willing buyer and seller, knowledgeable, not the fund itself
  • Measurement date: Fair value is specific to a point in time
The Fair Value Hierarchy

ASC 820 establishes a three-level hierarchy based on the observability of inputs:

LevelInput TypePE ExamplesFrequency in PE
Level 1Quoted prices in active markets for identical assetsPublicly traded portfolio company shares (post-IPO), liquid debt securitiesRare (~5% of PE portfolios)
Level 2Observable inputs other than Level 1 (quoted prices for similar assets, market corroborated inputs)Yield-based debt, recently priced leveraged loans, credit-bid situationsModerate (~20–30%)
Level 3Unobservable inputs based on entity's own assumptionsPrivate equity investments (buyout, growth, VC), illiquid debtDominant (~65–75%)

IPEV Guidelines

The International Private Equity and Venture Capital (IPEV) Valuation Guidelines provide practical guidance on how to apply fair value measurement to PE investments. They are not accounting standards themselves but are widely accepted as best practice by PE funds globally.

IPEV outlines the following valuation techniques in order of preference:

  1. Price of Recent Investment
  2. Multiples (EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, P/E)
  3. Net Assets (for asset-heavy companies)
  4. Discounted Cash Flows (DCF)
  5. Discounted Cash Flows from an Industry Participant
  6. Industry Valuation Benchmarks
  7. Available Market Prices
Valuation Methods for Private Investments
1. Market Approach — Comparable Company Multiples

The most commonly used approach for established, profitable businesses. Applies a valuation multiple derived from comparable public companies or recent M&A transactions to the portfolio company's financial metric.

Enterprise Value = EBITDA × EV/EBITDA Multiple Equity Value = Enterprise Value − Net Debt Example: Portfolio Company EBITDA: $25M Comparable companies trade at 8.5× EBITDA EV = $25M × 8.5 = $212.5M Less: Net Debt = ($65M) Equity Value = $147.5M

Selecting the right multiple requires judgment:

  • Use public company comps that are truly comparable (industry, size, growth, margins)
  • Apply a private company discount (illiquidity discount, control vs. minority)
  • Cross-check with recent M&A transaction multiples (often higher than public comps)
  • Consider company-specific factors (growth premium, distress discount)
2. Income Approach — Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

Values the company based on the present value of its projected future cash flows. More inputs, more assumptions, but useful when the company has a distinctive growth profile not reflected in comps.

Intrinsic Value = Σ [FCF_t / (1+WACC)^t] + Terminal Value / (1+WACC)^n Terminal Value (Gordon Growth): FCF_n × (1+g) / (WACC − g) Key inputs: • Free Cash Flow projections (5–10 year forecast) • WACC (weighted average cost of capital, typically 10–15% for PE) • Terminal growth rate (typically 2–3%) • Terminal multiple (cross-check)
DCF Sensitivity: A 1% change in WACC can change the valuation by 15–25%. A 0.5% change in terminal growth can shift value by 10–15%. Always run sensitivity tables and cross-check vs. comparables.
3. Price of Recent Investment

Shortly after a new investment (typically first 12 months if no significant events), cost may be the best indicator of fair value. However, this is not a permanent default — the fund must monitor for calibration triggers:

  • Significant changes in company performance vs. underwriting case
  • Market-wide multiple compression or expansion
  • New financing rounds at different valuations
  • Industry-specific events
4. Net Asset Value (Asset Approach)

Used for asset-holding companies (real estate, natural resources, holding companies). Values the sum of individual assets at their fair values, less liabilities.

Calibration

IPEV strongly recommends calibration — at the initial investment date, record the fair value technique and inputs that reconcile to the transaction price. Then at each subsequent measurement date, use those same inputs (adjusted for market changes) to assess whether FV has changed. Calibration creates an audit trail and defensible valuation process.

Valuation Governance & Process

Fair value governance matters as much as the valuation technique itself. Auditors, regulators, and sophisticated LPs all scrutinize the process as much as the number.

Valuation Committee

Most institutional PE funds have a Valuation Committee that meets quarterly to review and approve all investment fair values. Committee membership typically includes:

  • CFO or COO of the GP
  • Senior investment professionals (deal team)
  • Head of Finance / Fund Controller
  • Independent member (best practice)

The deal team proposes valuations; finance challenges and validates; the committee approves. This separation of duties is critical for audit purposes.

Third-Party Valuation Specialists

For large or complex Level 3 positions, GPs increasingly engage independent valuation firms to provide a fairness opinion or valuation conclusion. This is especially common for:

  • End-of-year (audited) valuations
  • Large unrealized positions ($50M+)
  • Distressed or turnaround investments
  • Early-stage venture investments
The Accounting Team's Role

As a PE fund accountant, your valuation responsibilities include:

  1. Collecting and organizing financial data from portfolio companies (monthly/quarterly)
  2. Preparing valuation workpapers (comp analysis, DCF models, FV schedules)
  3. Running sensitivity analysis and documenting assumptions
  4. Preparing Valuation Committee memos and supporting materials
  5. Recording approved FV marks as journal entries
  6. Preparing Level 3 rollforward disclosures for financial statements
  7. Responding to audit queries on fair value support
Career Tip: Being able to build and defend a valuation model — and articulate the key assumptions clearly — is one of the most valued skills in PE fund accounting. It moves you from "transactional accountant" to "trusted finance partner."
Carried Interest — The GP's Profit Share

Carried interest (or "carry") is the GP's share of the fund's profits. It is the primary economic incentive for the GP and aligns GP interests with LP interests — the GP only earns carry if LPs make money above the agreed hurdle rate.

The Four-Tier Waterfall

The standard waterfall distributes cash in four sequential tiers:

1
Return of Capital
LPs receive 100% of all contributed capital back (all capital calls funded, including amounts used to pay management fees). No profits yet — just getting invested capital back. GP co-invest capital also returned here.
2
Preferred Return (Hurdle)
LPs receive their preferred return — 8% per year compounded annually on contributed capital from the date of each call. The preferred return accrues from the call date, not the fund inception date.
3
GP Catch-Up
The GP receives 100% of distributions until it has received 20% of all profits distributed so far (the preferred return plus the catch-up). This "catches up" the GP so its share equals 20% of total profits.
4
Carried Interest Split
All remaining proceeds split: 80% to LPs, 20% to GP (carried interest). This continues for all remaining distributions.
Why Does the Hurdle Matter?
Example: $100M fund, $160M returned to LPs and GP combined Invested capital: $100M Preferred return (8% × 5 years, simplified): $46.9M total accrued Tier 1: LPs get $100M back (return of capital) Tier 2: LPs get $46.9M preferred return Tier 3: GP catch-up: 20% of ($100M + $46.9M) = $29.4M; GP gets 20% / 80% = GP gets ~$3.7M Tier 4: Remaining proceeds split 80/20 If fund returned only $120M total: LPs only receive return of capital + partial preferred return. GP gets zero carry.
Key Concept: Carry is only paid on profits above the hurdle. If the fund returns less than the preferred return threshold, the GP earns zero carried interest. This is why carry is a true incentive — the GP wins only when LPs win.
European Waterfall — Fund-as-a-Whole Model

The European waterfall is the most LP-friendly carry structure. It requires that the GP return all LP capital across the entire fund — including capital used to fund losing investments — before any carry is paid. ILPA endorses this model as best practice.

How European Waterfall Works
Key Principle: The fund is evaluated as one pool. Carry is only paid after:
(1) ALL LP contributed capital is returned (including from failed deals)
(2) ALL LPs have received their preferred return (8% p.a.) on ALL invested capital

This means early successful exits do NOT trigger carry — the GP must wait until the hurdle is met fund-wide.
European Waterfall — Full Example: Fund Size: $200M LP committed capital Investments: Deal A: $60M invested → $90M exit (+$30M gain) Deal B: $40M invested → $0 (complete loss) Deal C: $80M invested → $140M exit (+$60M gain) Total invested: $180M | Total returned: $230M | Net gain: $50M Step 1 — Return ALL contributed capital: LPs contributed: $200M (includes fees funded via capital calls) Must return full $200M before any carry Step 2 — Preferred return (8% × ~5 years, compounded): Approximate preferred return accrued: $46.9M Step 3 — Carry trigger: Total to LPs before carry: $200M + $46.9M = $246.9M Total fund proceeds: $230M In this example: proceeds < hurdle threshold → GP earns ZERO carry Modified example: Total returned = $280M Carry pool = $280M − $246.9M = $33.1M GP catch-up and carry: 20% of ($46.9M + $33.1M) = ~$16M GP carry
Why LPs Prefer European Waterfall
  • No early carry payments on winning deals while losing deals still exist
  • Eliminates most clawback risk (carry only paid when mathematically due)
  • Entire fund must succeed — GP incentivized to manage all deals, not just early winners
  • ILPA-endorsed: preferred by institutional LPs globally
Where It's Used

Dominant in European institutional PE funds, common in large global buyout funds, and increasingly adopted by US funds with sophisticated LP bases. The name "European" reflects its origin and prevalence in European markets, not a geographic restriction.

Remember: European waterfall = whole-of-fund carry. The GP waits. LPs protected first. This is now the institutional standard.
American Waterfall — Deal-by-Deal Model

The American waterfall pays carry on a per-deal basis — as each investment is exited profitably, the GP may receive carried interest on that deal's profits without waiting for the fund as a whole to return all capital.

How American Waterfall Works
Deal-by-Deal Waterfall — Example: Deal A: $60M invested → $90M exit Step 1: Return $60M capital on Deal A to LPs Step 2: Preferred return on $60M × 8% × hold period (e.g., 3 years) = ~$15.2M Step 3: GP catch-up and carry on Deal A's profits Carry pool (Deal A): $90M − $60M − $15.2M = $14.8M GP carry (20%): ~$2.96M paid NOW, before other deals exit Later: Deal B loses $40M Now LPs have: [$90M return - $60M capital - $15.2M pref] after Deal A = $14.8M gain But also $40M loss on Deal B Net to LPs: $14.8M gain - $40M loss = net loss! GP has already received $2.96M carry — this triggers CLAWBACK.
Clawback Mechanism

Because carry can be paid on winning deals before losing deals are known, the GP may receive more carry than it's entitled to on a fund-as-a-whole basis. The clawback provision requires the GP to return excess carry at fund wind-down.

Clawback Amount = Carry Received − Carry Earned (whole-of-fund basis) Key complications: 1. Tax: GP may have paid income tax on carry received → LPA often requires gross-up 2. Timing: Clawback only calculated at fund termination (may be 10+ years later) 3. GP solvency: What if the GP or individual carry recipients can't pay? 4. Escrow: LPs often negotiate carry escrow (20–30% held back) to secure clawback

Accounting for Carried Interest

How carry is recognized in the fund's books depends on whether it has been earned (based on current NAV) or paid (actual cash distribution to GP).

Accrued Carried Interest (Unrealized)

Under ASC 946, accrued carry is typically recognized as an allocation of partners' capital when it is attributable based on current NAV — even before cash is distributed. This is known as the "catch-up" allocation approach or the income allocation approach.

GP's carry allocation in financial statements: If current NAV and distributions, when taken together, would trigger carry payment, allocate the carry portion to the GP capital account: DR Partners' Capital — LPs (carry re-allocation) $X CR Partners' Capital — GP (accrued carry) $X
Two Schools of Thought on Carry Presentation
MethodDescriptionCommon Usage
Capital Account MethodCarry is an allocation to GP capital account (no separate expense line). It reclassifies partners' capital from LPs to GP.Most US PE funds
Expense MethodCarry is shown as an incentive fee expense on the income statement before calculating net income attributable to LPs.Some hedge funds; rare in PE
Clawback Liability

If carry has been paid (cash out the door) but the fund's performance subsequently deteriorates, the GP may have a clawback obligation. This should be disclosed in the financial statement notes and may require a contingent liability accrual if the clawback is probable and estimable.

Carry Escrow

Many LPAs require a carried interest escrow — typically 20–30% of carry distributions are held in escrow until fund wind-down to protect LPs against clawback. From an accounting perspective:

On carry payment with escrow: DR Partners' Capital — GP $2,000,000 [total carry] CR Cash (distributed to GP) $1,600,000 [80%] CR Escrow Holdback Payable / Restricted Cash $400,000 [20% escrowed]
GP Commit vs. Carry — Don't Confuse Them
GP Capital Commitment: The GP's pro-rata investment in the fund (1–3%). This is co-investment capital, just like LP capital. It earns returns and bears losses proportionally.

Carried Interest: The additional profit allocation (20%) earned by the GP for managing the fund successfully. This is separate from GP co-invest returns.

A GP capital account combines both components — GP co-invest NAV + accrued carry.
Clawback Provisions — Protecting LP Returns

A clawback is the GP's contractual obligation to return previously distributed carry to LPs if, at the end of the fund's life, the GP has received more carry than it was entitled to on a fund-as-a-whole basis.

When Does Clawback Occur?

Clawback arises when:

  • Early exits generate profits → carry paid to GP
  • Later investments lose money → overall fund returns fall below hurdle
  • Result: GP received carry it wasn't mathematically entitled to

This is most common in American (deal-by-deal) waterfall funds. European waterfall funds almost never trigger clawback because carry is only paid after the full fund hurdle is met.

Clawback Calculation Example
Fund total LP capital contributed: $100M Preferred return (8% × 5yr): $46.9M Total LP return needed before any GP profit: $146.9M Total fund distributions to LPs + GP: $160M Total carry paid to GP during fund life: $8M Total distributed to LPs: $152M Was LP hurdle met? $152M vs. required $146.9M → LPs received MORE than hurdle → No clawback needed Modified: LPs only received $140M Shortfall: $146.9M − $140M = $6.9M GP must return min($8M carry, $6.9M shortfall) = $6.9M clawback due to LPs
Clawback Complications
  • Tax gross-up: GP paid income tax on carry received. LPA may require GP to return pre-tax amount (effectively reimbursing tax already paid). LPs in different tax positions may benefit differently.
  • Individual vs. fund-level: Carry is often paid to individual deal team members. If the fund claws back, individual professionals must return personally received carry — a significant personal financial burden.
  • Solvency risk: 10 years after carry was paid, can the GP or individuals actually return it? Carry escrow mitigates this.
  • Legal disputes: Clawback calculations can be complex and contested. Legal fees can erode the amount actually returned.
Escrow as Mitigation

The most effective protection is a carry escrow: the fund holds back 20–30% of each carry payment until fund wind-down. If clawback is due, it comes from escrow first. ILPA recommends carry escrow be required in all LPAs.

Core Performance Metrics — IRR & MOIC

PE performance is measured using a small set of standard metrics that every fund reports to LPs. Understanding these metrics — how they are calculated, their strengths, and their limitations — is essential for any PE accountant.

IRR — Internal Rate of Return

IRR is the annualized rate of return that makes the Net Present Value (NPV) of all cash flows (in and out) equal to zero. It accounts for the time value of money — a dollar returned in Year 1 is more valuable than a dollar returned in Year 5.

NPV = 0 = Σ [CF_t / (1 + IRR)^t] where CF_t = cash flow at time t Negative = capital calls (money out from LP perspective) Positive = distributions (money back to LP) Example: Year 0: −$1,000,000 (LP capital called) Year 1: −$500,000 (additional call) Year 3: +$800,000 (partial distribution) Year 5: +$2,000,000 (exit distribution) Solve for IRR: approximately 22.4% per year
XIRR vs. IRR

Standard IRR assumes regular periodic cash flows. PE cash flows are irregular (random dates). XIRR handles actual calendar dates — it is the correct calculation for PE.

XIRR in Excel: =XIRR(values, dates) =XIRR({-1000000, -500000, 800000, 2000000}, {1/1/2020, 6/15/2020, 3/20/2022, 9/30/2024}) Always use XIRR for PE. Standard IRR gives incorrect results when cash flows are irregular.
MOIC — Multiple on Invested Capital

MOIC (also called TVPI — Total Value to Paid-In) is a simple multiple: how many times the LP's money has been returned. It does NOT account for time value of money.

MOIC = TVPI = (Distributions + Remaining NAV) / Total Capital Contributed Example: Total capital called: $100M Distributions paid: $80M Remaining NAV: $60M MOIC = ($80M + $60M) / $100M = 1.40× Interpretation: LPs have gotten back 1.40× their money so far (realized + unrealized)
IRR vs. MOIC — Use Both
ScenarioIRRMOICLesson
$1M → $2M in 1 year100%2.0×High IRR, low absolute gain
$1M → $2M in 5 years15%2.0×Same MOIC, much lower IRR
$100M → $200M in 5 years15%2.0×Same ratios, 100× more dollars
$100M → $400M in 10 years15%4.0×Low IRR, great MOIC
Rule of Thumb: IRR tells you speed; MOIC tells you magnitude. You need both. A 50% IRR on $1M over 6 months is less impressive than a 20% IRR on $500M over 7 years.
DPI, RVPI & TVPI — Return Multiples

These three ratios are components of the same performance story: how much of a fund's value is realized (cash in hand) vs. unrealized (paper gains).

The Three Multiples
DPI — Distributions to Paid-In
DPI = Total Distributions / Total Capital Called (Paid-In Capital) DPI measures what LPs have actually received in cash. DPI = 1.0× means LPs got their money back DPI > 1.0× means LPs are in profit on a cash basis
RVPI — Residual Value to Paid-In
RVPI = Remaining NAV / Total Capital Called RVPI measures the unrealized value still in the fund. Early in fund life: RVPI is high, DPI is low Late in fund life: RVPI should be declining as exits occur
TVPI — Total Value to Paid-In
TVPI = DPI + RVPI = (Distributions + NAV) / Paid-In Capital This is the same as MOIC.
Reading the Multiples Together

0.0×1.3×1.3×Early-stage fund — all paper gains, no cash returned
0.8×0.9×1.7×Mid-life fund — some exits, healthy unrealized

Paid-In Capital (the Denominator)

Paid-In Capital (PIC) is total LP capital actually called and funded — not the total commitment. This distinction matters:

Total LP Commitment: $200M Paid-In Capital (cumulative calls): $175M Unfunded: $25M Multiples use $175M (Paid-In), not $200M (Commitment) Using the wrong denominator is a common error in LP reporting

Never confuse gross and net returns. A fund might show 25% gross IRR but 18% net IRR after fees and carry. LPs evaluate net returns. Always label clearly which basis you're using.

Financial Highlights Disclosure

Under ASC 946, investment companies are required to disclose financial highlights in their financial statements. For PE funds, this typically includes:

  • Per-unit (or per-interest) data: contributions, distributions, change in NAV
  • Total return (IRR at the fund level)
  • Ratios: expense ratio (fund-level expenses / average NAV)
J-Curve Effect

PE fund returns typically show a characteristic pattern known as the J-Curve:

Years 1–3 (Investment Period): IRR is negative or low. Capital is deployed (cash out), management fees reduce NAV, early investments haven't yet appreciated.

Years 4–6 (Value Creation): Unrealized appreciation builds as portfolio companies grow. IRR turns positive.

Years 7–10 (Harvest Period): Exits generate realized cash distributions. DPI rises. IRR converges to final return.

This J-curve shape is why early-vintage IRRs are not comparable to mature fund IRRs.
ILPA Reporting Standards

The Institutional Limited Partners Association (ILPA) is the primary industry body representing LP interests. ILPA has published standardized reporting templates and principles that have become the de facto global standard for PE fund reporting.

ILPA Principles Overview

ILPA's three core principles for PE fund governance:

1
Alignment of Interest
GP and LP economics should be structured so both benefit from fund success. Includes GP co-investment requirements (1–3%), management fee offsets, fair hurdle rates, and European waterfall.
2
Governance
LPs should have meaningful oversight rights: LP Advisory Committee (LPAC), key man provisions, no-fault divorce right, audited financials, independent valuation processes.
3
Transparency
GPs should provide comprehensive, timely reporting using standardized templates. No hidden fees, clear disclosure of all GP economics.

The LPAC is a subset of major LPs who represent the LP community in governance matters. The LPAC reviews and approves:

Capital Call Process
1

GP issues capital call notice (typically 10 business days before funding date). Notice includes: dollar amount for each LP, purpose (investment, fees, expenses), funding date and wire instructions.
2

LPs wire their pro-rata share to the fund's bank account. Pro-rata = LP's commitment as % of total fund commitments (or per LPA).

Funds wired to target company or used for management fees, as specified in the call notice. Subsequent entries record the investment or expense.

1
Trigger Event
Portfolio company exits (sale, IPO, dividend recapitalization). GP receives proceeds in fund's bank account.
2

Finance team calculates the waterfall (per LPA) to determine how proceeds are split between return of capital, preferred return, GP catch-up, and LP/GP carry split.
3

UBTI — Unrelated Business Taxable Income

Tax-exempt LPs (pension funds, endowments) are generally not taxable on investment income. However, UBTI — income from businesses "unrelated" to their exempt purpose — may be taxable. UBTI typically arises from:

  • Debt-financed investments (through leverage at the fund or portfolio company level)
  • Operating business income (e.g., portfolio companies that generate "active" income)

Tax-exempt LPs carefully track UBTI exposure. Many use offshore blockers (Cayman subsidiaries) to shield UBTI.

ECI — Effectively Connected Income

Non-US LPs may be subject to US tax on ECI — income effectively connected with a US trade or business. This is particularly relevant for funds with US portfolio companies. Non-US LPs often invest through "blocker" vehicles to avoid ECI exposure.

Career Tip: Tax is a specialized area. PE fund accounting teams work closely with tax advisors and fund administrators on K-1 preparation. Understanding the GAAP-to-tax reconciliation and LP-specific tax concerns (UBTI, ECI) makes you a more valuable team member.
Income & Loss Allocation Methods

Allocating the fund's income, gains, losses, and expenses to individual LP capital accounts is one of the most technically demanding areas of PE fund accounting. The allocation method must follow the LPA and be applied consistently.

Target Capital Account Method

The most commonly used method for PE funds. Each LP's capital account should always reflect what they would receive if the fund were liquidated at current fair values — based on the LPA's distribution waterfall.

Step 1: Determine what each partner SHOULD have in their capital account if the fund were liquidated at current FV values (run the waterfall calculation with current NAV) Step 2: Calculate the required change to get from current balance to target: Allocation = Target Capital Account − Current Capital Account Step 3: Book that difference as the period's income/loss allocation This ensures capital accounts always track the waterfall at every reporting date.
Pro-Rata Method

Simpler approach: allocate all income and loss based on each partner's percentage ownership (their capital commitment as a % of total). This works well when there are no complex waterfall features (no carry, no multiple LP classes).

Pro-Rata Allocation: LP A: 60% commitment → receives 60% of all income/loss/expenses LP B: 30% commitment → receives 30% GP: 10% commitment → receives 10% Limitation: Doesn't handle carry, preferred return, or different LP terms correctly.
Layered Allocation Approach

When there are multiple LP classes (Class A, Class B) with different fee rates or carry rates, allocation happens in layers:

1
Class-Specific Allocations
Items that belong specifically to one class (class-specific management fees, carry allocations for that class) are allocated only to that class.
2
Common Pool Allocation
Remaining income/loss shared among all classes in proportion to their capital account balances.
3
True-Up at Year-End
Run the full waterfall calculation to verify each LP's capital account matches their liquidation entitlement. Adjust if needed.

The management fee is NOT the only cost to the fund. LPs bear additional fund-level expenses:

  • Tax preparation (Form 1065, K-1 preparation)
  • Legal fees (ongoing fund legal counsel)
  • Fund administrator fees (if third-party)
  • LP advisory committee expenses
  • D&O insurance and fund insurance
Fund expenses are typically capped at 0.1–0.25% of fund size per year. Expenses above the cap are borne by the GP. As accountant, monitor expenses against the cap and flag any overages to the GP.
Capital Call Mechanics & Recycling
How Capital Calls Work in Practice

When the GP needs capital for a new investment or to cover fund expenses, it issues a capital call notice. Each LP funds their pro-rata share based on their commitment percentage.

Example Capital Call: GP needs $12,000,000 for new investment + $500,000 quarterly management fee Total call: $12,500,000 LP Commitment Percentages: LP A (commitment $100M): 40.0% → $5,000,000 LP B (commitment $75M): 30.0% → $3,750,000 LP C (commitment $50M): 20.0% → $2,500,000 GP (commitment $25M): 10.0% → $1,250,000 Total: 100.0% → $12,500,000
Recycling of Capital

Capital recycling allows the GP to re-invest early distributions back into new investments, effectively deploying more than the fund's total committed capital over the fund's life. Not all LPAs permit recycling — it must be specifically allowed.

Example of recycling: Fund commitment: $100M (all called) Year 3: Company A exits → $20M returned to LPs as return of capital Fund value after exit: $80M in remaining investments With recycling: GP can re-call the $20M returned to fund new investments Total deployed over fund life: up to $120M ($100M + $20M recycled) Without recycling: Capital is permanently returned; fund can only invest from original $100M
Subscription Lines of Credit

Many funds use short-term credit facilities (subscription lines, also called "sub lines" or "capital call facilities") to bridge the time between signing a deal and calling capital from LPs. The credit line is secured by unfunded LP commitments.

Purpose: Close deals quickly without waiting 10 days for LP capital calls Typical term: 90–180 days Interest rate: SOFR + 1.5–2.5% Accounting for sub-line usage: On draw: DR Investment / Cash $15,000,000 CR Subscription Line Payable $15,000,000 On capital call (LPs fund): DR Cash $15,100,000 [principal + interest] CR Partners' Capital — LPs $15,000,000 CR Interest Expense $100,000 [Then repay the line] DR Subscription Line Payable $15,000,000 CR Cash $15,000,000
IRR Impact: Subscription lines boost reported IRR because they delay LP capital calls. A fund may show a 3–5% higher IRR by using sub lines compared to calling capital at deal close. LPs increasingly require "unlevered IRR" (calculated from deal date, not call date) to compare funds fairly.
Consolidation & Investment Entity Exemption
The Consolidation Question

PE funds typically own majority (controlling) stakes in portfolio companies. Under general consolidation principles (ASC 810 / IFRS 10), controlling ownership would require full consolidation of portfolio companies into the fund's financial statements. However, this would make PE fund financial statements completely uninterpretable — imagine consolidating 15 different operating companies into one set of financial statements.

US GAAP — Investment Company Exception (ASC 946)

ASC 946-810 provides that entities that qualify as investment companies do NOT consolidate their portfolio company investments. Instead, they measure them at fair value through the income statement. This exception applies even if the fund owns 100% of a portfolio company.

Key Rule: A PE fund that qualifies as an investment company under ASC 946 is EXEMPT from consolidation requirements of ASC 810. Each portfolio company stays as a line item on the Schedule of Investments at fair value — no full consolidation.
IFRS — Investment Entity Exemption (IFRS 10)

IFRS 10 defines an investment entity and provides a similar exemption. To qualify as an investment entity under IFRS 10, the entity must:

  • Obtain funds from investors to provide investment management services
  • Commit to investors that its business purpose is investing for returns (capital appreciation, investment income, or both)
  • Measure and evaluate performance of substantially all its investments on a fair value basis

PE funds meeting these criteria measure portfolio company investments at FVTPL (Fair Value Through Profit or Loss) rather than consolidating them.

Exception to the Exception

If a subsidiary provides investment-related services to the fund (e.g., a management company subsidiary), that subsidiary IS consolidated into the investment entity's financial statements. Portfolio company operating subsidiaries are still measured at FV, not consolidated.

Practical Impact for Accountants
With ConsolidationWithout Consolidation (PE)

Secondaries & Co-Investments
Secondary Transactions

Secondary transactions involve the sale and purchase of existing PE fund interests between investors — rather than buying new portfolio companies directly. This provides liquidity in an otherwise illiquid asset class.

LP-Led Secondaries

An LP sells their interest in an existing fund to a secondary buyer at a negotiated price (usually a discount to NAV). The fund itself is unchanged; only the LP register changes.

Accounting for LP secondary transfer (fund records): 1. LP A transfers their interest to Secondary Buyer for $18M (LP A's capital account was $20M — sold at 90% of NAV) 2. Fund records: DR Partners' Capital — LP A $20,000,000 [remove at book value] CR Partners' Capital — Buyer $20,000,000 [add new LP at book value] Note: The fund records the BOOK VALUE transfer. The $2M discount (LP A's loss) is between LP A and the buyer — not the fund's transaction.
GP-Led Secondaries (Continuation Funds)

A newer structure where the GP transfers assets from an aging fund into a new "continuation fund" to extend the hold period on high-performing assets. Existing LPs can cash out or roll into the new vehicle.

Accounting challenge: GP-led secondaries involve related-party conflicts (GP on both sides of the transaction). LPAC approval required. Independent fairness opinion needed. NAV at transfer date must be independently validated.
Co-Investments

LPs are often offered the opportunity to invest directly alongside the fund in specific portfolio companies. This is called a co-investment. Co-investments typically have:

  • No management fees (or reduced, e.g., 0–0.5%)
  • No carried interest (or reduced, e.g., 5–10%)
  • Direct ownership of a stake in the specific company

Co-investments are attractive to large LPs (pensions, SWFs) who want to increase their PE exposure at lower cost.

Accounting for Co-Investments

Co-investments are typically structured as separate vehicles (SPVs) parallel to the main fund. Each SPV has its own set of books:

Structure: Main Fund → 80% of Company X equity Co-Invest SPV → 20% of Company X equity (LP A and LP B directly) Co-Invest SPV journal entries are identical to main fund but on a smaller scale: - Investment purchase at cost - Quarterly FV marks - Distribution on exit - Capital account statements for each co-investor
NAV Facility Lending

A newer financing innovation: lenders extend credit to PE funds secured by the NAV of the entire portfolio (not just LP commitments like sub-lines). Used to fund distributions, GP liquidity, or bridge capital calls. Accounting similar to sub-lines but longer duration and more complex covenants.

  • Approval workflows for journal entries, wire releases, and valuation memos

Regulatory Environment for PE Funds
US Regulatory Framework
Investment Advisers Act of 1940

Most PE fund GPs managing more than $150M in assets are registered investment advisers (RIAs) with the SEC. SEC registration requires:

  • Annual Form ADV filing (disclosure of business, fees, conflicts)
  • Written compliance policies and procedures
  • Chief Compliance Officer (CCO) designation
  • SEC examination readiness
Dodd-Frank Act (2010)

Removed the "private adviser" exemption that previously allowed PE funds to avoid SEC registration. Required most PE GPs to register as RIAs. Also introduced LP right to know about side letters and fee reporting requirements.

Form PF

Large private fund advisers (>$2B AUM) file Form PF with the SEC quarterly, providing systemic risk data: fund size, leverage, concentration, liquidity. Accounting teams support Form PF data compilation.

European Regulatory Framework
AIFMD — Alternative Investment Fund Managers Directive

EU regulation requiring European fund managers (and non-EU managers marketing in Europe) to register as Alternative Investment Fund Managers (AIFMs). Requirements include:

  • Depositary appointment (independent asset safekeeping oversight)
  • Annual AIFMD report to regulators
  • Remuneration policies and disclosure
  • Leverage limits for certain fund types
ILPA Fee Reporting (US) and AIFMD Annex IV (EU)

Both require standardized fee and expense disclosure. As accountant, you will compile data for these reports.

ASC 740 — Tax Uncertainty

PE funds must evaluate whether uncertain tax positions meet the "more-likely-than-not" threshold for recognition. Key PE tax uncertainty areas: carried interest treatment under Section 1061, UBTI characterization, and state tax nexus for funds investing across multiple states.
